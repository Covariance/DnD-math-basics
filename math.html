Math Basics¶

This section can be skipped at any moment if you feel bored, however, it is probably important to understand other topics to their fullest.

Let’s give you small excursion into discrete probability theory. There are events that have probabilities. We will mostly talk about independant events, which do not affect each other, such as two consequent dice rolls. In our case, event is a value that you rolled on your dice. For example, probability of rolling \(20\) on a d20 equals to \(1/20 = 0.05\).

Expectation¶

Expectation is actually the mean value that you will get while rolling your dice and is defined by following formula:

\[ E(a) = \sum j \cdot p(j)\text{,}\]

where \(E(a)\) is expectation, \(j\) is value and \(p(j)\) is probability of rolling corresponding value.

To grasp the idea behind expectation you can imagine, that we make a lot of dice rolls and take their mean value. It is most likely to be very close to the expectation. That means, that bigger expectation gives better results in a long run.

Example

Let’s calculate an expectation of a single roll of an N-sided die, a.k.a. dN:

\[ E(dN) = \sum_{j=1}^N j \cdot p(j) = \left(\sum_{j=1}^N j \right) \cdot \frac{1}{N} = \frac{(N \cdot (N+1))}{2\cdot N} = \frac{N+1}{2}\]

Variance¶

There’s also one very important value — variance, which defines, how consistent the random event would be and is calculated by the following formula:

\[ Var(a) = E((a-E(a))^2) = E(a^2) - E(a)^2 \]

The idea behind variance is pretty straightforward — the bigger it is, the more inconsistent our random value becomes. For example, constant value has a variance of \(0\), and d100 has a variance of \(833,25\).

Example

Again, I will calculate variance of a single roll of an N-sided dice, a.k.a. dN:

\[\begin{gather*} E(a^2) = \sum_{j=1}^N j^2 \cdot p(j) = \left(\sum_{j=1}^N j^2\right) \cdot \frac{1}{N} = \frac{N\cdot(N+1)\cdot(2N+1)}{6\cdot N} = \frac{(N+1)\cdot(2N+1)}{6}\\ E(a)^2 = \left(\frac{N+1}{2}\right)^2\\ Var(a) = E(a^2) - E(a)^2 = \frac{(N^2-1)}{12} \end{gather*}\]

Thus, for d20 expectation equals to \(10.5\) and deviation equals to \(33.25\).

All this formulas will be useful later. Let’s get started.
